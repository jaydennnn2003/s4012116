Techniques
The exposure triangle is a popular representation of the three variables that determine the exposure of a photograph: aperture, shutter speed, and ISO. To achieve the desired outcome, all three must be balanced, with changes to one necessitating changes to at least one of the others. They influence not only exposure but are also the most important determinants of an image's overall appearance. To gain am image with the correct exposure all three must be adjusted and set to account for the other settings brightness.
Shutterspeed
Shutter speed is the length of time the camera shutter is open, exposing light onto the camera sensor. Essentially, it’s how long your camera spends taking a photo. This has a few important effects on how your images will appear.

When you use a long shutter speed (also known as a “slow” shutter speed), you end up exposing your sensor for a significant period of time. The first big effect of it is motion blur. If your shutter speed is long, moving subjects in your photo will appear blurred along the direction of motion. To overcome this issue smaller lenses are usually used to reduce the effect of movement an alternataive for when larger lenses are required is a tripod. Tripods provide a stabalized platform for the camera to sit on greatly minimalizing motion blur. Another issue faced is that photos seem too appear brighter. This is due to the sensor experiencing too much light as the lens is open for too long. Ensuring the ISO is at its lowest and the aperture is at its highest setting allows the main lens to be open for longer and be exposed with less light. Shutterspeed effects are used quite often to capture an image in motion, where a sense of speed and motion is communicated to the viewer by intentionally blurring the moving wheels.

ISO
In very basic terms, ISO is simply a camera setting that will brighten or darken a photo. As you increase your ISO number, your photos will grow progressively brighter. For that reason, ISO can help you capture images in darker environments and allows more flexibility with aperture and shutter speed settings.

Common ISO values for cameras today range from:
  • 100 - lowest
  • 200
  • 400
  • 800
  • 1600
  • 3200
  • 6400 - highest
the higher the value of the ISO the more grainy the photo will turn out. to reduce the amount of grain or noise within a photo either increase the shutter speed or decreas the aperture. Aperture
The opening in a lens through which light passes to enter the camera is referred to as an aperture. It's a simple concept to understand if you consider how your eyes work. The iris in your eyes expands or contracts as you move between bright and dark places, controlling the size of your pupil. The aperture is the term given to the lens's "pupil" in photography. You can change the aperture size to allow more or less light to reach your camera sensor. Aperture is an adjustable lens opening that controls the amount of light that enters the camera. An f-stop number, often known as an f-number, is used to calculate aperture. The greater the number, the smaller the aperture, resulting in less light reaching the lens. The lower the f stop number, the higher the number. The f-number often ranges from f/1.4, which allows for more light exposure, to f/16, which allows for the least amount of light exposure. f/16 lenses are developed for landscape, architecture, and macro photography when a lot of depth of field is required.
